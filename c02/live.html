Seattle's CO2 Measured at the Space Needle

Last 3 Days

This shows data collected every 5 minutes over the last 72 hours on the Space Needle. Variations in CO2 are related to human activities in and around the city (number of cars on the road, gas heating and cooking, etc.) as well as the local weather patterns (windy weather will blow locally produced CO2 away).

This Month

This chart shows hourly averaged CO2 data collected over the last 30 days. The CO2 values over Seattle are higher and more variable because many of our daily activities such as cooking with gas or driving to work release CO2 into the atmosphere.

Seasonal

This shows daily averaged data collected over the last few years. Having monitors in a major city and in an unpopulated area lets us compare how human activity and natural forces impact atmospheric CO2 levels.

With monitors on the Space Needle and on a buoy off the Washington coast near Aberdeen, we can observe how our regional CO2 levels vary on a regular basis.

In February of 2010, scientists at NOAA's Pacific Marine Environmental Laboratory, in collaboration with the Pacific Science Center and Seattle's Space Needle, installed an instrument to measure how much CO2 is in the air over Seattle.
